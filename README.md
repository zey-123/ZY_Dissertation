**Project title:** Phenological Shifts in Plankton Blooms at the Bermuda Atlantic Time-series Study (BATS) Site: Exploring the Role of Sea Temperature Change 

**Brief Description of Project and Methods:**

This project investigates how long-term ocean warming is influencing the seasonal dynamics (phenology) of plankton communities in the open ocean, focusing on the Bermuda Atlantic Time-series Study (BATS) site. Plankton—comprising phytoplankton (photosynthetic organisms) and zooplankton (their primary consumers)—are foundational to marine food webs and highly sensitive to environmental changes, especially temperature. 

As climate change intensifies, understanding how these organisms respond in terms of bloom timing, magnitude, and duration is critical for predicting broader ecosystem impacts. Located ~75 km southeast of Bermuda, the BATS site in the subtropical North Atlantic Ocean represents one of the longest-running and most comprehensive open-ocean ecological time-series globally. Since 1988, BATS has collected bi-weekly to monthly data on a suite of physical, chemical, and biological variables, offering a rare opportunity to examine long-term ecological responses to climate drivers in a relatively undisturbed, open-ocean context. Unlike coastal datasets (e.g., Continuous Plankton Recorder data), BATS avoids many anthropogenic influences, allowing clearer attribution of changes to climate variability and long-term trends such as ocean warming.

The core aim of this research is to examine whether phenological shifts—such as changes in the onset, duration, and peak timing of plankton blooms—can be detected at BATS and whether these shifts correlate with rising sea surface temperatures and its indirect effects of stratification. Specifically, the project will test two sets of hypotheses:
1.	Warming seas have altered the phenology of phytoplankton, leading to earlier or prolonged bloom periods.
2.	Warming and stratification has also shifted the phenology of zooplankton, potentially resulting in temporal mismatches between primary producers and consumers.

The analysis will proceed in two main stages. First, phenological indices will be calculated for both phytoplankton and zooplankton using monthly abundance data (following transformation of raw data). These indices—such as bloom onset, peak timing, duration, and amplitude—will be derived using established methods from the literature. For phytoplankton, chlorophyll-a concentrations will serve as a proxy for biomass. For zooplankton, direct abundance records will be used.

Second, the study will apply time-series and statistical analyses to assess how these indices vary over the 30+ year dataset. This will include linear trend analysis, Generalized Linear Models (GLMs), and repeated-measures ANOVAs to test for temporal changes and linkages to sea surface temperature. The strength of this approach lies in its ability to condense complex, multi-species data into interpretable phenological trends that can be quantitatively compared across years and taxa.

By focusing on long-term phenological patterns and their link to environmental parameters of temperature and stratification, this research addresses a key gap in the literature: the lack of open-ocean, long-term analyses of plankton seasonality under climate change. The results will enhance our understanding of ecosystem sensitivity to warming oceans, and may provide insights into broader implications for oceanic carbon cycling, trophic mismatches, and marine biodiversity.
